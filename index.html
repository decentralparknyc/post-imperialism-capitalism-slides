What Kinds of Goods Exist?

Capitalism is optimized for one quadrant. What about the others?

Public Goods

Non-rivalrous + Non-excludable. Everyone benefits, no one is excluded.

Examples: National defense, street lighting, open-source software, mathematical knowledge, clean air

Markets systematically underprovide these (free rider problem)

Club Goods

Non-rivalrous + Excludable. Access restricted, but use doesn't diminish supply.

Examples: Netflix, toll roads, cinemas, gyms, encrypted WiFi, gated communities

Requires artificial exclusion mechanisms (DRM, paywalls)

Common-Pool Resources

Rivalrous + Non-excludable. Open access, but consumption depletes.

Examples: Ocean fisheries, groundwater, forests, the atmosphere, wild game

"Tragedy of the Commons" — Hardin (1968)

Private Goods

Rivalrous + Excludable. Capitalism's sweet spot.

Examples: Food, clothing, cars, houses, smartphones, oil

Price signals, profit motive, property rights all work here

Capitalism Works For One Quadrant

The entire market mechanism — price signals, profit motive, private property, competition — is optimized for rivalrous + excludable goods. It encounters systematic failures everywhere else:

  • Public goods: Underprovided (no profit incentive)
  • Commons: Over-exploited (tragedy of the commons)
  • Club goods: Require artificial scarcity (DRM, IP law)
Samuelson (1954), Musgrave (1959), Buchanan (1965), Ostrom (1990 — Nobel 2009)
Stanford Encyclopedia: Public Goods

The Digital Shift

Digital goods (software, data, AI models, knowledge) are inherently non-rivalrous — copying costs nothing. As Jeremy Rifkin argues, capitalism's internal contradiction is that competitive markets drive marginal costs to zero, but capitalism requires scarcity to generate profit.

Part I — Post-Capitalism

What Is Post-Capitalism?

Collective ownership, no state reliance, better for everyone

A post-capitalist mechanism satisfies all three criteria:

1

Collective ownership without any state means — not state socialism, not charity, not custodial

2

Better than the capitalist alternative for both consumers and workers

3

Survives without charity or custody — structurally incentivized, not dependent on altruism

Example: Wikipedia

The canonical post-capitalist organization. Paul Mason, Yochai Benkler, and Michel Bauwens all cite it as a working model of commons-based peer production (CBPP).

  • Collectively owned — no shareholders, no CEO, governed by community
  • Better for consumers — free, comprehensive, available in 300+ languages
  • Better for contributors — intrinsic motivation, no wage labor exploitation
  • No state involvement — Wikimedia Foundation is a nonprofit, not a government entity
  • Not charity — structurally self-sustaining through voluntary donations and volunteer contributions
Commons-based peer production • Paul Mason: PostCapitalism (2015)

Example: The Mutual Vending Machine

A collectively-owned vending machine where the community owns the hardware and inventory. Profits go back to the collective rather than a private operator. No landlord, no franchise fees, no corporate extraction.

  • Lower prices for consumers (no profit margin extracted)
  • Revenue funds community initiatives instead of shareholder returns
  • Maintenance and restocking organized by members cooperatively

Applying the Model: The Mutual Laundromat

The same vending machine model scales to a collectively-owned laundromat:

  • No landlord extraction: Community owns or collectively leases the space
  • No franchise fees: Machines purchased collectively, not leased from a corporation
  • Lower prices: Cost-recovery pricing instead of profit-maximizing pricing
  • Better maintenance: Users have direct stake in upkeep; no absentee owner neglect
  • Revenue stays local: Surplus funds community programs, not distant investors
  • Democratic governance: Members vote on hours, pricing, improvements

A capitalist laundromat extracts ~30-40% profit margin. A mutual laundromat returns that margin to users as lower prices or reinvests in the community.

Part II — Post-Imperialism

What Is a Post-Imperialist Mechanism?

Three criteria a system must satisfy

1

Stable Under Attack

Sufficiently stable over time even under sustained attack from massive imperialist countries. Cannot be shut down, co-opted, or destroyed by nation-states.

2

Provides a Service

Sufficiently provides a meaningful service that persists over time, even under sustained attack. The service remains available regardless of geopolitical pressure.

3

No State Reliance

Runs autonomously without any state involvement. No government operates it, funds it, or can shut it down. It is truly sovereign from the nation-state system.

Why These Criteria Matter

Imperialism operates through the monopoly of violence (Lazzarato) and the violent appropriation of means of production (Luxemburg). A post-imperialist mechanism must therefore be fundamentally resistant to violence, coercion, and state capture. It must be antifragile to imperial pressure — not merely surviving attacks, but becoming more distributed and resilient in response to them.

This is a radically different framing from post-capitalism, which primarily asks "can we organize production differently?" Post-imperialism asks: "can we build infrastructure that empires cannot destroy?"

Utopian Socialism: A Brief History

Beautiful visions, fatal vulnerabilities

"Any general character, from the best to the worst, from the most ignorant to the most enlightened, may be given to any community, even to the world at large, by the application of proper means." — Robert Owen, A New View of Society
"Under civilization poverty is born of superabundance itself." — Charles Fourier
"The golden age of the human race is not behind us, it is at the forefront, it is in the perfection of the social order." — Henri de Saint-Simon
"The solution of the social problems, which as yet lay hidden in undeveloped economic conditions, the Utopians attempted to evolve out of the human brain." — Friedrich Engels, Socialism: Utopian and Scientific (1880)

The Core Problem

Engels' critique: utopian socialists rejected class struggle and revolutionary action, hoping to transform society through peaceful persuasion and model experiments. They appealed to all humanity at once rather than organizing the proletariat as a revolutionary class.

But the deeper problem is defense. Every utopian community that survived did so by being too small to threaten existing power. When socialist projects posed a genuine threat — the Paris Commune (1871), revolutionary Spain (1936), Allende's Chile (1973) — they were crushed by military force.

Engels: Socialism Utopian & Scientific, Ch. 1

Key Figures

  • Robert Owen (1771-1858) — Welsh industrialist. Proved humane conditions + profitability at New Lanark. Founded New Harmony, Indiana (collapsed in 2 years). Lost 80% of his fortune.
  • Charles Fourier (1772-1837) — Proposed "phalanxes" of ~1,600 people. Rejected work-as-virtue. Proto-feminist. 25+ communities attempted, almost all failed within 2-5 years.
  • Henri de Saint-Simon (1760-1825) — Envisioned governance by scientists and industrialists. Coined "administration of things" (later adopted by Marx).
  • Etienne Cabet — "Icarian" communes in TX, IL, IA, CA (1848-1890s). Plagued by internal disputes.

The Inescapable Trap

As the Marxist critique established: you cannot build a new society in the cracks of the old one without confronting the old one's monopoly on violence. Beyond the Binary

If imperialism never achieves global hegemony, there are other options

The Key Assumption

The dialogue tree assumes you must either acquire weapons or submit. But this only holds if imperialism achieves total global hegemony. Historically, it never has. There have always been zones outside direct imperial control — ungoverned spaces, neutral territories, failed states, digital frontiers, international waters.

The Expand / Recede Model

Instead of standing and fighting (acquiring nuclear arms) or submitting to an imperial patron, a network of autonomous collectives can expand into uncontested zones and recede from zones under imperial pressure.

  • No fixed territory to defend — the network is nomadic and digital-first
  • Physical nodes (co-living, co-working) exist as an archipelago across jurisdictions
  • When one node is threatened, assets and operations shift to others
  • The network grows by establishing new nodes, not by defending borders
  • Like water, it flows around obstacles rather than breaking through them

Insurance Against Imperialist Expansion

The network doesn't just run — it actively insures itself against imperial incursion into its zones:

3
Automatic asset liquidation — DeFi protocols convert assets to portable stablecoins, bridge to accessible chains
4
Relocation fund release — Pre-funded multi-sig wallet releases funds for movement to a new zone

Every component exists in production today: TLSNotary, Reclaim Protocol, Etherisc, Aave/Uniswap, cross-chain bridges.

Part IV — P2P Sanctions

p2p2p: Peer to Peer to Peace

Decentralized economic cooperation between citizens of conflicting nations

The Core Thesis

Traditional sanctions are blunt instruments imposed top-down by powerful nations. They harm civilians, lack transparency, shift with political winds, and reinforce imperial power dynamics.

p2p2p flips this: citizens of Country A and Country B create mutual economic interdependence through a shared token system. Peace becomes profitable. Conflict becomes costly. At the individual level.

The Dual-Token Model

Two tokens — one for each nation — backed by respective national currencies:

  • Verified citizens mint at 1:1 rate (deposit $100, get 100 tokens)
  • Global supporters mint at premium rate (1:0.8) — the 20% premium funds the peace incentive pool
  • Both tokens governed at the municipal level
  • Both subject to the same redistribution rules
  • Vision: decades-long gradual integration of the two tokens as trust builds

Game Theory: Engineered Negative-Sum Conflict

The system creates a Lose/Lose outcome for conflict. If Country A attacks Country B:

  • Country A token holders lose tokens (redistributed to Country B holders)
  • Country A businesses lose access to Country B markets
  • Country A's token value drops as the network shrinks
  • Individual Country A citizens pay the cost, not just the state

Peace is the only Nash equilibrium — the only strategy where no participant benefits from deviating.

Black Wednesday: How Soros Broke the Pound

September 16, 1992. George Soros, Stanley Druckenmiller, and Scott Bessent identified that Britain had joined the European Exchange Rate Mechanism at an overvalued rate of 2.95 DM per pound.

  • Built a $10 billion short position against the pound
  • Risk-reward was asymmetric: max downside ~0.5%, potential upside 15-20%
  • Bank of England spent £27 billion defending the peg — failed
  • Raised interest rates from 10% → 12% → 15% in a single day — failed
  • UK withdrew from the ERM at 7:30 PM

Result: Soros profited ~$1.8 billion. The pound fell 15% against the DM and 25% against the dollar. The Conservatives lost credibility and didn't return to power for 18 years.

Collectivizing the Mechanism

What if the same economic pressure could be applied in a decentralized and anonymous way?

  • Morris & Shin (1998) proved that coordinated attacks don't need explicit coordination. When participants have similar private information, a unique equilibrium emerges where each independently attacks at the same threshold.
  • GameStop (2021) proved retail investors coordinating on Reddit can execute institutional-grade strategies without any central leader.
  • Terra/UST (2022) demonstrated a Soros-style attack on a crypto peg, destroying $45B in one week.
